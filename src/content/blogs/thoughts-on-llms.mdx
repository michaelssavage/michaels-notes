> AI can make you go faster. But sometimes you go fast in the wrong direction and it causes more issues than not.

I'm impressed by the reasoning of Large Language Models (LLMs) and the output of Generative AI tools that speed up code development quite often, but there's the ever-present feeling that it's (I'm?) destroying the world. I'm not unique in my feelings about AI, as theres incredibly cynical debates daily on social media platforms like Reddit, Twitter, and <Anchor link="https://news.ycombinator.com/news" text="Hacker News" variant="link" isExternal />, the all-things-computers social site.

> Over reliance on the *black box of bullshit* is going to become such a disaster in a way no one would have thought possible. Not sure exactly what, who, when, or where... Just that having your entire product or repo dependent on a single entity is going to lead to some bad times...

The above comment is very doom and gloom. You see this a LOT online. For some, the whole thing can be seen as a money racket that happens to be useful now and again. Back in 2019, OpenAI said that their new natural language model, GPT-2, was too dangerous to release. They then said the 64x64 image model DALL-E too scary, and their researchers were **starting to panic**. This can be decent storytelling for investors and shareholders. Google too, the worlds largest search engine, stated on May 19th, 2026 that they are entering a new era for AI search, putting their 'advanced model capabilities' right at your fingertips. Investors are licking their lips as they can now use this intelligent Search box to ask questions like "How many P's are there in Google?". The below screenshot is from May 28th, 2026.

<Picture src="/blog/search-result.jpg" alt="How many P's are there in Google? Exactly one apparently." style={{ margin: "0 0 1rem" }} />

The CEO of Meta, Mark Zuckerberg, has acknowledged that there are growing concerns about "low-quality content, aka AI slop" on Instagram and Facebook. He said his team is working on ways to improve systems to find and remove "low quality, repetitive content". This AI slop is being created specifically to deceive people, but it's hard to see how this backlash will be resolved while it still fuels shareholder value.

Gallup writes, "While a steady 51% of Gen Zers in the U.S. report using generative artificial intelligence at least weekly, <Anchor link="https://news.gallup.com/poll/708224/gen-adoption-steady-skepticism-climbs.aspx" text="negative emotions toward it have intensified over the past year" variant="link" isExternal />. Anger about the technology has risen, while excitement and hopefulness have dropped". 

As a software developer, I use AI pretty much daily. Especially around November 2025, coding agents started becoming more reliable and hallucinating less, going from working on a whim to working most of the time. They crossed a quality barrier where I could start using them as a daily-driver to get real work done, without needing to spend most of my time fixing their mistakes. But everything AI outputs still needs to be reviewed, though. It is still just a tool.

I use Cursor as my code editor, Claude for agentic coding tasks, and ChatGPT from time to time for general-purpose tasks. I've certainly started projects that I wouldn't have started without AI, and I've made progress on topics that I would have really struggled with. <Anchor link="/worksheets" text="The Spanish Worksheets project" variant="link" /> that I have been building for the past few months is a good example of this. It involves scheduling tasks, generating content with AI, and sending emails programmatically, and immediate feedback on correct answers. I studied computer applications in University, and I think this project would have been a lot harder had I not had this strong foundation in software development already. 

One of the best frontend developer teachers to learn from is **joshwcomeau.com**. In one of his blog posts, he writes about his opinions of <Anchor text="AI in a positive light" link="https://www.joshwcomeau.com/email/wham-launch-005-elephant-2-p/" variant="link" isExternal />. Those who are highly technical will benefit hugely from AI. At the end of the day it is only a tool, one that tends to get personified. He suggests that AI has a multiplying effect on our existing technical skills. Contrast that to a gardener who's spade amplifies the gardener's ability to do their job, only our techno-spade is sycophantic and oddly says "you're right" sometimes. 

> AI is allowing new beginners to become developers and create projects. But it's breaking the process that allowed developers to create intricate work like LLMs. It's preventing the middle rung in the ladder of development. 

Straight back to the negativity; Futurism writes that <Anchor text="'this tech is dismantling the career ladder before you can even step onto it'" link="https://futurism.com/artificial-intelligence/gen-z-attitude-ai" variant="link" isExternal />. You can send thousands of job applications into an AI void while CEOs gloat that they'll replace workers and weaken their bargaining power. This rhetoric suggests that entry-level workers are being pushed towards a permanent underclass. Engineers still need to learn to program, learn to create design systems, and organize code, but the milestones of adulthood keep drifting ever-further out of reach. The Harvard Gazette quoted an <Anchor link="https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2025/11/is-ai-dulling-our-minds/" text="MIT Media Lab study" variant="link" isExternal />, reporting that “excessive reliance on AI-driven solutions” may contribute to “cognitive atrophy” and shrinking of critical thinking abilities. The biggest source code platform for storing, managing, and collaborating, GitHub, is being inundated with AI-generated code. Everyone can be a developer now, so there's the issue of people mindlessly creating mountains of slop, causing developer jobs to be perceived as less valuable.

> Coding with AI agents is just gambling. You get a fantastic result once in a while and you think they're magical. You end up chasing that high, tweaking the prompt parameters to take on bigger tasks and creating more chaotic superstitions.

King's College London's Institute for Artificial Intelligence and Policy Institute asked 2,000 UK adults aged 16+ questions in April 2026. From that sample size, there was some interesting feedback. <Anchor text="Seven in ten of the UK public are worried about the economic impacts of AI" link="https://www.kcl.ac.uk/news/one-in-five-britons-think-ai-will-create-civil-unrest-study-finds" variant="link" isExternal />, six in ten think it will eliminate more jobs than it creates, half think its impact will be worse than a normal recession, and one in five think it will create civil unrest. Public appetite for government intervention is strong. Two-thirds (66%) say AI companies should be closely regulated even if it slows development.

Pope Leo XIV has even lent his opinion on the topic of AI in his encyclical, "Magnifica Humanitas". He writes that the technology is not a force antagonistic to humanity nor is it inherently evil, but it is never neutral. He continues to say that the guiding principle shouldn't be profit but the dignity of every person and the common good of all people. A poignant thought from the encyclical is that <Anchor text="the rising technocratic paradigm threatens humanity's freedoms and dignities." link="https://www.vaticannews.va/en/pope/news/2026-05/pope-leo-xiv-encyclical-magnifica-humanitas-ai.html" variant="link" isExternal /> Now, I'm not one to quote The Pope too often, but that's a thought that'll make you thunk.

As this document continues to be edited and rewritten, I end on this one request. Stop copying and pasting AI responses into conversations with people. It's so profoundly saddening to ask an adult a question and receive an AI answer. It's like calling your Mam to answer for you. If you're able, be a self-sufficient entity.
