> AI can make you go faster. But sometimes you go fast in the wrong direction and it causes more issues than not.

My thoughts on Large Language Models (LLMs) are changing constantly. Quite often actually, I'm impressed by the output of Generative AI tools that speed up my development, but there's the ever-present feeling that it's (I'm?) destroying the world. I'm not alone about my feelings of AI, as there's daily debates on social media platforms like Reddit, Twitter, which is unsurprisingly cynical, and <Anchor link="https://news.ycombinator.com/news" text="Hacker News" variant="link" isExternal />, the all-things-computers social site.

<Anchor link="https://news.gallup.com/poll/708224/gen-adoption-steady-skepticism-climbs.aspx" text="This article" variant="link" isExternal /> from Gallup writes "While a steady 51% of Gen Zers in the U.S. report using generative artificial intelligence at least weekly, negative emotions toward it have intensified over the past year. Anger about the technology has risen, while excitement and hopefulness have dropped". 

As a software developer, I use AI pretty much daily. I use Cursor as my code editor, Claude for agentic coding tasks, and ChatGPT from time to time for general purpose tasks. I've certainly started projects that I wouldn't have started without AI, and I've made progress on topics that I would have really struggled with. <Anchor link="/worksheets" text="The Spanish Worksheets project" variant="link" /> that I have been building for the past few months is a good example of this. It involves scheduling tasks, generating content with AI, and sending emails programmatically. I studied computer applications in University, and think anyone with a foundation in software development can really push themselves further with these tools.

Especially around November 2025, coding agents started becoming more reliable, going from working on a whim to working most of the time. They crossed a quality barrier where you could start using them as a daily-driver to get real work done, without needing to spend most of your time fixing their mistakes. But I believe everything AI outputs still needs to be reviewed though.

> AI is allowing new beginners to become developers and create projects. But it's breaking the process that allowed developers to create intricate work like LLMs. It's preventing the middle rung in the ladder of development. 

Futurism writes about the sentiment, <Anchor text="'this tech is dismantling the career ladder before you can even step on'" link="https://futurism.com/artificial-intelligence/gen-z-attitude-ai" variant="link" isExternal />. You can send 1000s of job applications into an AI void while CEOs gloat that they'll reduce you to a permanent underclass. Engineers still need to learn to program, learn to create plans, and organize code. Are we watching the milestones of adulthood drift ever-further out of reach?

From a different angle, the Harvard Gazette quoted an <Anchor link="https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2025/11/is-ai-dulling-our-minds/" text="MIT Media Lab study" variant="link" isExternal />, reporting that “excessive reliance on AI-driven solutions” may contribute to “cognitive atrophy” and shrinking of critical thinking abilities. GitHub, the biggest source code platform for storing, managing, and collaborating, is being inundated with AI-generated code. Everyone is a developer now really, and everyone is creating mountains of slop while not really using their brain.

> Over reliance on the *black box of bullshit* is going to become such a disaster in a way no one would have thought possible. Not sure exactly what, who, when, or where... Just that having your entire product or repo dependent on a single entity is going to lead to some bad times...

For some, the whole thing can be seen as a money racket that happens to be useful now and again. Starting seven years ago, OpenAI said that their new natural language model, GPT-2, was too dangerous to release. They then said the 64x64 image model DALL-E too scary, and their researchers were starting to panic. They were right unintentionally.

The CEO of Meta, Mark Zuckerberg, has acknowledged that there are growing concerns about "low-quality content, aka AI slop". He said his team is working on ways to improve systems to find and remove "low quality, repetitive content". AI slop has been created specifically to deceive, and it is rightfully angering people, who are in turn, spurring a new wave of backlash against AI.

> Coding agents is just gambling. You get a fantastic result once in a while and you think they're magical. You end up chasing that high, tweaking the prompt parameters to take on bigger tasks and creating more chaotic superstitions.
