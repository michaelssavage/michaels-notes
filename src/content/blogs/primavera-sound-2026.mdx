A jumble of music genres, a salmagundi of vendor plates, and a ragbag bunch of international punters gathered for a weekend in Europe's definite Coachella-adjacent event. Grab all the synonyms for a 'mixed bag' and they'll work for Primavera Sound. Nearly 300,000 people stepped onto the Parc del Fòrum grounds during this weekend. Concrete in place of desert surroundings, the diverse crowd battled harsh rain and wind on the Thursday, to be rewarded with a dry and sunny weekend of joy and great memories.

Primavera Sound runs from Thursday to Saturday, with many enticing add-ons in the weeks leading up to the main event that enhance the exclusivity of the event and a final farewell show on the Sunday called Primavera Bits. My first taster was on the Monday at a Primavera a la Ciutat gig. An off-site ticketed event in Sala Apolo highlighting two incredible guitar players in distinct styles: Gwenifer Raymond and Yerai Cortés. Gwenifer played a raw style of guitar strumming inspired by blues and Appalachian folk music. After, the room filled out for Yerai Cortés and the six accompanying singers. They performed an amazing flamenco show with a theatrical modern twist. I haven't heard much like it and was voracious for every beat.

On the Wednesday, as part of the Primavera Opening Day, the crowd was offered a sneak peek of the venue, watching Guitarricadelafuente and Wet Leg in a confined part of the grounds. The event was in hot hot demand since it was free. You just needed quick fingers in the ticket queue and a deposit to secure your spot. Both the Monday and Wednesday events required a deposit, but because I attended the shows, I got my money back. Can't complain about free gigs! I skipped the Sunday Primavera Bits show, as my body was in bits and pieces.

The festival, overall, was tough going. It has some of the best artists in the world coming to Catalunya, which means set times overlap, which means lots of dancing to your favourite songs, and it means outlandishly late nights. I didn't live near the grounds, Parc del Fòrum, so the journey home took more than an hour each night. I was forewarned about the late nights followed by late sleep-ins, so I had duly booked time off work.

The full-experience ticket cost a right penny. I bought an instalment plan during the general sale, and it cost me €350 + booking fees. The most I've ever spent on any festival or music event. I suppose you could waver your thinking and rationalise that a Bad Bunny or Taylor Swift ticket alone is €200, so surely it's actually cheap, right? The psychological value of Primavera Sound is increased by its price tag. Many people are attending, but you feel part of an exclusive club. The price of food and drink was another hard-hitter. €6 for a 500ml beer and €12 to €15 for any sort of meal really wracked up the costs quickly. Can't complain about free gigs, though, righttt?!

The grounds were heavily branded, and it was quite nauseating to move a couple of meters and be blasted with ugly signs from yellow aviation *BRAND NAME*, edgy bank *BRAND NAME*, three-stripes *BRAND NAME*, and so on. It's a capitalist festival, it's the opposite of counterculture, and it's pretty fucking ugly without the people. Excuse my language.

But as much as I'd like to lambaste consumer society, I was a part of it, and all us consumers were friendly. The people did not withhold their praise, care, or appreciativeness for each other, and everyone I met was in the best mood and super excited to meet new people. The majority of people spoke English (anecdotally), and there was a great Catalan presence in the crowd speaking their native language, which I attempted a few times. The crowd-goers were super fashionable, and it was the number one charming feature of the festival for me. I empathised a tiny bit for those that dressed well on the Thursday to just conceal their outfits under a plastic poncho.

Primavera Sound gets a lot of slack for attracting 'posers' and influencers. Vibe-chasers who get caught up in the image rather than the music, recapping the events on TikTok and Instagram. I really didn't see much of this on the grounds. Phones, for the most part, were tucked away. There were a few photo opps and what-not, but it was pretty timid. My subjective experience may not be reality for others because I went to more DJs and rock bands, which may be more rowdy and less phone-friendly. Staying on the topic of the people, I found the crowds easy to manoeuvre. No stage was overcrowded for me, and I felt like I had plenty of space to dance (badly). 

Overall, the weekend was jam-packed with excellent music. The vibes were great throughout and I felt safe. The Cupra Pulse stage and Plenitude stage were electronic music-based, and I highly enjoyed my time there the most. It was so enjoyable, I bought my ticket for next year on the presale. Oops!

# Below are my standout shows of the week.

## Yerai Cortés

Powerful, demanding of silence, theatrical, beautiful, big & bold. Attention and admiration were rightfully directed towards the six girls on stage who synchronously clapped, sang, and performed flowy movements with the mics. Cortés and the crowd praised them over and over, but I don't know their name.

## Guitarricadelafuente

A crowd-pleasing performance on the opening day on the Occident stage. Many of the crowd were singing along and dancing energetically to the songs. Barcelona approves of him. I approve of him.

## Wet Leg

The last set on opening day, this band from the Isle of Wight always gives their best, but it seemed like the crowd was too self-restrained, thinking about how to get home and reserve their energy levels for the coming weekend.

## Men I Trust

My first official act on the main timetable. Great to watch the band play *Show Me How* on the Cupra stage from a distance. Unfamiliar with most of their music, but the saccharine beats melted the crowd. A pleasant surprise.

## Geese

Awesome band. I was ecstatic to hear the American Rock band live for the first time. Top of the radar for thousands of attendees, even causing bit of a ruckus on the day before when queues vaporized for Cameron Winter's solo set. Good vibes on the Occident stage but I had to leave early as the rain started to pummel down.

## Mac Demarco

FML. I didn't actually see Mac perform, as the Occident stage was cancelled due to the rain and wind. Gutted.

## Ahadadream

I had a clash in scheduling. THe predicament was to either go to TV Girl in the rain like the Geese gig or head over to Plenitude to see Ahadadream? I rallied the troops at midnight, and we stomped over to the high-energy DJ. It was a good shout. Plenitude was a smaller stage with a great sound system and lightshows.

## Role Model

Role Model was not on my radar, but after countless mentions from people's must-see lists, I checked out the country-twang pop star. Lead singer Tucker Harrington Pillsbury wooed the younger crowd, mostly girls, with his charisma and slick moves while a demolition derby crash compilation played on the big screen.

## Skrillex

I got up close to the stage for Skrillex. I didn't know what to expect. Was he going to play tunes from when I was fifteen or his newer modern sound? A bit of both, it seemed. I didn't have much time to self-reflect because the pyrotechnic flames from the stage heated up my face, and my cheeks were vibrating from the ginormous speakers. It was a very busy set with Latin-infused elements, dubstep, and dance tracks like *Make It Bun Dem*. It's what the people wanted.

## Viagra boys

Believe me, this is one of the best bands in the world. The Swedish punk band played on the Occident stage to an eager audience. I secured access to the mosh-pit frenzy, which topped the list of the performances I saw over the weekend. They played all the greats like *"Punk Rock Loser"*, *"Uno II"*, and *"Troglodyte"*.

## Big Thief

I slowly rose to life on the Saturday, the last day, by sitting on the fake grass at the main stage, watching Big Thief. The sun had started to set at this time, and Adrianne Lenneker radiated the right energy out so that I could enjoy the full night. It was a beautiful gig, and I can't wait to see her perform *"Not"* again.

## Lil Simz / The XX

Most of the day was spent at the main stage. With no acts to frantically run to, I watched Lil Simz and her live band, from afar, blend hip-hop, jazz, and soul together. They were awesome! Some oohs and aahs as she came out in a Spanish Football jersey, but she was forgiven for providing funky rhythms galore. From the same position, I watched The XX come on stage and get everyone off their tushes. Vocals from Jamie XX and Romy sounded insanely good from the huge sound system as they played *"Crystalized"* and *"Loud Places"*.

## Four Tet

After word got out earlier in the day about the curation of the Cupra Pulse stage by Skrillex, I had no choice but to run for the Four Tet set. Luckily, I waltzed right into the pit, with hundreds having to queue mere minutes after. Four Tet's DJ set followed by a B2B with Skrillex was everything I wanted to finish the weekend. Highly skilled DJs throwing down the best bass music in a surround-sound arena. Unbelievable.
