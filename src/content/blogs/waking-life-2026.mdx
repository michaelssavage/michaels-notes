A Tolstoyan strategy is taking life fifteen minutes at a time. A blissfully unexamined life from birth to death. A Socratic life is the opposite. Commercial festivals are experienced in small units in a Tolstoyan narrative. You go from stage to stage, ignoring the abstract elements along the way, seeing as many acts as possible and maximizing the schedule. Waking life casually breaks down this ignorance by not giving out a schedule of the artists ahead of arrival. Festival-goers and I had no idea where to position ourselves at any one point during the festival, and first-timers had no idea what any landmarks would be. Not known to me at the time, but I would be liberated from **passionate desire**, which pressures us to think no more than fifteen minutes ahead. What stage do I need to be at? What artist can I not miss out on? Waking Life forced me to do nothing and learn.

The festival is a midsummer music festival run by a Portuguese not-for-profit association, situated in Crato, two hours outside of Lisbon, Portugal. Surrounding the idyllic Nascentes Dam, it runs for one week with music, art, workshops, and pop-up performances. In their own words, they describe the week better than I ever will: 


> "…an invitation to participate in a new pedagogy of revelry. An inquiry into what maturing hedonism looks like, or how embodied revolt feels. It can take the shape of a rite-of-passage or a bad trip. It may be an auditory feast or an erotic expansion."

For the first time in my life, I felt like I was part of a truly special human festival, a transient spiritual communion. The delightful mix of Portuguese, English, Spanish, French, Dutch, Italians and other languages I didn't know, made the weekend feel like a celebration stuck in some ancient moment in time. It was like an auspicious feast that celebrated creating things of extraordinary beauty. The community had a huge range in ages and people from walks of life, and I felt like I was on the younger side of the median age, which was initially a bit intimidating. But after the first morning, I started developing a sense of belonging. Waking Life, for me, was a unifying event that walked between the past and present, finding inspiration from the natural and digital world. Looking at the attention to detail on their website <Anchor link="https://2026.wakinglife.pt" text="2026.wakinglife.pt" variant="link" isExternal /> and <Anchor link="https://2025.wakinglife.pt" text="2025.wakinglife.pt" variant="link" isExternal /> was a foretaste of the craftsmanship.

More like a personal journey than a standard music festival, I got my boots rocked after coming from Primavera Sound only two weeks prior. Waking Life presents itself in a magical way that is interpreted differently by everyone. Getting bored felt like the goal because it meant looking at the soft details and having deeper conversations. On the minute scale, you had silly posters with nonsense text like *"Has anyone really been far even as decided to use even go want to do look more like?"* and on the grander scale, you had sacred spaces like the song dome that you had to crawl into. This was a reverent no-alcohol wigwam near the Mimo stage where you could listen to spoken word and acoustic singing. The festival did these big and small elements equally well.Beside my pitched tent there was a creative camp called Eden Cinema that ran movies at night time on a projector. On my way to sleep on the last night, I ended up watching Cabaret in this relaxing area.

I wasn't a witness to many phones being used, which made it all seem timeless. The phones were specifically pointed at as a bad thing, a devil-ish thing that had to be kept off the dancefloor at all times. Not going to lie, it felt cult-y. I'm all for not using your phone where people are dancing, but I already can't recall many of the tracks that were played at the weekend. So, I don't have a list of tracks that I can go back to next week or the next week after that, and relive the smaller moments. I digress to an event I went to years ago, where I took out my phone to record a ten-second snippet of a song I really liked, only to be interrupted by an *inebriated* person "telling me to live in the moment". I guarantee you that person didn't remember half of that night, never mind the song. I know I'm on my high horse, but I can refresh my memory of that night with that clip.

Each person is given a little handy festival bible which contains guidance on who the music artists are, what stages exist, little adventures you can carry out, and on how to embrace weirdness. The booklet kept egging on about how life is strange, and I tried to embrace this weirdness without really knowing what that meant. I didn't wear unusual clothes or have a weird background. But what I did well, was practice gratitude and set my intentions. I was thankful to the lake for giving reprieve from the sun, the secret hideout spots that were meticulously created, the multiple creative camps that permitted me to paint and bring out my inner child, and to the strangers who asked me unconventional conversation starters. At times, I felt fragile, but accepting the little bit of weirdness helped to take me out of my funk. Below is a simple drawing painted with beetroot paint as part of a pop-up in the casa marmelada.

It hit 37°C or 38°C during the days, and apparently 43°C on Sunday in nearby areas. Unbearable hotness and dust meant a feeling of disgust during the days. For some people, this detriment collapsed their positive thinking. You can't see the best in your fellow people when you aren't hydrated or sleeping enough. It was clear that there were revellers who did not look after their own body, first and foremost. After the festival ended, I read Reddit reviews from far too many negative people who may have been taking pleasure in their own sadness. Stroking their own egos, having superiority complexes over other people's sexuality, where they were from, or their drug choices. Get a f******g grip. The festival was made up of so many beautiful uplifting souls, in my opinion, because of the way I set my intentions and viewed my surroundings. I got the energy that I put out there.

I met many Kiwis, Aussies, British, and even Chinese travellers for the sole purpose of attending Waking Life. I met a few fellow Irish folk, had nice conversations with people from London and Cologne, and shared jokes with anyone who would listen. Shout-out to the group Dan, Liam, and Mylo, who adopted me and my travel buddy / best pal Peter.

The festival is care-free in one fascinating element: There are lots of naked bodies from all genders. It didn't matter. Showing skin did not matter. Nude bodies danced and swam, and were simply not lusted for during the day-to-day runnings. For those who did find a sexual attraction, they could go to a dedicated safe place to have sex. This area, the Suna area, was run by professionals who ran workshops about practical sex tips, knowing more about sex workers, fetishes, and erotic play setups. Nudity was explored here without shame or guilt. Even though it wasn't an area that I ventured to, it showed me that the festival gave responsibility and respect to the attendee.

As someone who thought they knew electronic music, I got a swift reminder of the Dunning-Kruger effect. The DJs that played in Crato were real crate-diggers and played fantastic tunes that neither I nor Shazam knew about. Very few commercial tracks ever played, and I really lagged behind in knowing what genres were being played at times. The stages could sort-of be split up with cutesy name tags by their genre offerings. Cochilo had live instrumental and vocal music, Outro Lado had house and tech-house music, always four-to-the-floor, Floresta had bass music and techno, Praia had breaks, dub music, and drum'n'bass, and Mimo had alternative, experimental, and ambient works. The genres were not isolated and would often blur into one another so that groups that identified most with a style of music would hear something new.

I hadn't an inkling of an idea about most of the tracks played during the weekend. I spent long stretches of time at the praia stage with Lisbon-based o ghettao, DJ Caring, and Nightmares on Wax. There were some moments of pure glee like hearing Flight FM by Joy Orbison, spirits lifting when a Music Sounds Better With You remix was played at Praia, with the lyrics "I feel better, the music sounds right," and the most utterly bizarre moment of the festival had to be during the Rhadoo set. The Romanian producer played a track called Black Naga by the Pachanga Boys. A freaky track that had alien-sounding gibberish that excelled because of how good the sound systems were. In fact, each stage had exceptional sound design. The engineers did a top-notch job delivering high-quality sound whether it was the natural amphitheatre of Mimo or the Dub Sound Systems brought to Praia.

The Cochilo stage was fantastic the whole time I went past. A band called Holy Tongue had me saying holy f*** with their crazy live electronic setup, and a Ghanaian band, Ata Kak, had the whole crowd grinning playing their happy sounds for most people's final sunset on Sunday. They reminded me of When The Going is Smooth & Good by William Onyeabor.

Music exploration is something I've always prepped before going to festivals. I sit down with headphones ritualistically, look up a snippet about the artist, and mindfully listen to what they're doing. A part of me still wants to contest whether or not knowing the lineup dampened my experience, but post-festival, I think it truly was for the best. It's really just the ego that says "I must have", "I need", and "I want", and I never would have embraced the music in the moment if I had known who was DJing ahead of time. In fact, I never saw what any of the DJs looked like anyway, so it wouldn't have mattered. Instead I did a lot of wandering around to the different stages, and as mentioned already, it made the brain wander. I switched off the part of my consumer society brain that had to be at the next gig. The music never stopped and the crowds only ever thinned down to a few numbers, never was there an empty dancefloor.

Waking Life is trying to convey the importance of sustainability. The people in charge and volunteers mean well by protecting trees and plants from campers, highlighting the biodiversity that lives near the lake, and trying to get you to understand your impact on the grounds. They run land regeneration projects and operate as a "year-round dojo for creation".

A small token of ecological responsibility that every attendee ends up being a part of, is eating the on-site food. All the food was vegetarian. You could eat delicious pizzas, mushroom burgers, gyozas, curries, falafels, and more cuisines without worrying about eating meat. There was delicious fruit on sale too, with oranges stands and watermelon slices for sale.

There was a lot of good drink to pair with the food that seemed reasonably priced. Cans of Sagres cost 3.75€, cocktails like espresso martinis from 12€, wine from 4€, hard seltzers from 4.75€, and many non-alcoholic offerings of coconut water, ginger sodas, mango sodas, and coffee. Water stations were spread out galore across the site and it was not uncommon to get a surprise shower by someone wielding a garden water sprayer.

"You have to realize you're dreaming in the first place. You gotta be able to recognize it. You have to be able to ask yourself, "hey man, is this a dream?". Most people never ask themselves that, when they're awake or especially when they're asleep. It seems like everyone is sleepwalking through their waking state and wake-walking through their dreams. Either way they are not going to get much out of it." This is a quote from the movie <Anchor link="https://www.alphavillejournal.com/Issue8/HTML/ArticleKulezicWilson.html" text="Waking Life" variant="link" isExternal /> (2001).

The festival has a lot of parallels to the movie of the same name, which explores reality, drifting from place to place, letting things flow like a dream, and blurring boundaries. You believe in their programming, you question things with an open-mind, and you are present. Although at times, it felt like a test of self-control and preservation in the insane heat, I have never been so eager to write my thoughts down after an event. It was one of the best festivals I've attended. Waking Life is Socratic.
